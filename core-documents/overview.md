# The Candor Project

## The Core Problem Worth Solving

The US is experiencing a structural failure of epistemic accountability — meaning there is no institution whose job it is to ensure that people in power pay a meaningful cost for stating empirically false things in their official capacities. Social media algorithms have made this worse by rewarding outrage over accuracy, and both political and media incentives have adapted accordingly. The problem isn't new but it has accelerated to a point where the normal self-correcting mechanisms of democracy — a free press, an informed electorate, competitive elections — are no longer sufficient on their own.

## What the Solution Needs to Be

Based on everything we've discussed, any viable solution needs to satisfy several design principles simultaneously:

- It must be constitutionally defensible, meaning it can survive First Amendment challenges
- It must be structurally independent from all existing branches of government
- It must be narrow in mandate — focused on empirically falsifiable factual claims, not opinion, not political judgment, not policy disagreement
- It must have consequences with teeth that don't require criminalizing speech
- It must be resistant to capture by any political faction
- It must be bipartisan in design so it can survive changes in political power

## The Two-Track Framework

### Track One: Politicians

This is your stronger ground legally and politically. The proposal is an enforceable federal ethics regime focused specifically on:

_Who it covers:_ The President, Vice President, Cabinet secretaries, all Senators and Representatives, federal judges, and Supreme Court justices.

_What it covers:_ Statements made in official capacities — floor speeches, press briefings, State of the Union addresses, media appearances made in their official role, sworn and unsworn public testimony.

_What counts as a violation:_ Empirically falsifiable statements of fact that are documented as false — not broken campaign promises, not opinions, not value judgments, not claims in genuinely contested scientific territory. The standard would be something like "knew or reasonably should have known this was false given their access to information."

_Consequences:_ Not criminal prosecution, which is constitutionally fraught, but professional disqualification — after a documented threshold of violations, a politician becomes ineligible to hold federal office. This treats truth-telling as a condition of professional conduct rather than a criminal matter.

### Track Two: Media

This is harder terrain constitutionally and should be pursued separately and more modestly. The most defensible interventions are:

_For public broadcasters_ (NPR, PBS, and equivalents): Binding editorial standards, mandatory correction policies, and clear opinion/news labeling as conditions of federal funding. This is the cleanest legal ground available.

_For licensed broadcasters:_ FCC licensing conditions requiring standardized, publicly accessible corrections records and mandatory on-air labeling of opinion programming. Not content regulation — condition of licensure.

_For all outlets:_ Disclosure requirements around ownership, funding sources, and political donations by parent companies. Modeled on existing sunshine laws, courts have generally upheld disclosure requirements.

The media track deliberately avoids direct content regulation and focuses on transparency and accountability infrastructure instead.

## The Oversight Architecture

The mechanism that makes both tracks work is an independent epistemic oversight body. Its design principles, drawn from what survived our discussion:

_Constitutionally established_, not just legislated, so it can't be dismantled by a simple congressional majority. This requires a constitutional amendment — a generational project, but the right architecture.

_Funding independence_ — a constitutionally mandated funding mechanism not subject to annual congressional appropriation, similar to how some countries protect judicial budgets.

_Appointment independence_ — staggered long terms, supermajority confirmation requirements across multiple bodies, explicit exclusion of current or recent politicians, party officials, and media executives.

_Staffed from academia_ but not run by academia — commissioners are drawn from relevant expert communities through a structured transparent process, but operate independently once appointed. This captures academic expertise and peer review methodology without the political baggage of universities as institutions.

_Narrow mandate_ — it produces a legally admissible public record of documented false statements. It does not impose punishments directly. Its findings feed into the disqualification mechanism for politicians and the licensing conditions for media. Its power is reputational and procedural.

_Methodological standard_ — findings must meet something equivalent to peer review standards: supported by established empirical evidence, subject to appeal, published transparently. The institution enshrines a methodology, not an ideology.

## The Mutual Accountability Structure

Rather than one body policing everything, the system works through overlapping accountability:

- The epistemic oversight body documents false statements and maintains the public record
- Congress retains oversight of public media funding conditions
- The press retains its existing First Amendment role of holding politicians accountable
- The epistemic body's findings are an input into existing mechanisms — ethics committees, disqualification proceedings, licensing reviews — rather than replacing them

No single institution controls the others. Each has a defined lane. The system is designed so that capturing one body doesn't capture the whole apparatus.

## What This Movement Should Lead With

If you're building a coalition around this, the most politically durable framing is probably not "we want to punish liars" — that's easy to caricature. The stronger framing is "people in positions of public trust should be held to the same standard of factual accountability as anyone else in a professional role." A doctor can lose their license for documented negligence. A lawyer can be disbarred for knowingly making false statements to a court. A financial advisor can be sanctioned for misrepresenting facts to clients. The idea that elected officials and licensed broadcasters should face no equivalent professional standard is the anomaly, not the reform.

That framing is harder to argue against publicly, travels across partisan lines, and focuses the conversation on professional accountability rather than government control of speech — which is exactly the distinction your proposal needs to maintain to survive.
